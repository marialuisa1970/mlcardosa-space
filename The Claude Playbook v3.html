Start here · Read this first

Most people use AI
the wrong way.

They open a fresh chat, type a vague question, and wonder why the answer feels generic. The issue is rarely the model: it is the setup. Before you build anything, internalise these three ideas.

The real problem

A well-designed project changes everything.

A well-designed project gives Claude a role, a goal, and clear rules, so responses stop drifting, stop being too long, and start feeling like they were written for you.

This playbook shows you how to build one. We will use a real worked example: a Job Coach project for your job search. By the end, you will have a reusable template and four copy-paste prompts.

"A good project doesn't try to do everything. It does a few important things very well."
Principle one of three

Role before task.

Tell Claude who it is before you tell it what to do. "Act as my Job Coach for the DACH market" unlocks sharper answers than "help me with jobs." The role gives Claude a frame of reference for every response it writes.

"You would not brief a consultant without explaining who they are working for, what they know, and what success looks like. Same logic applies here."
Principle two of three

Context beats cleverness.

Your country, target roles, languages, what you don't want: context is free and it is the single biggest quality lever you have. A prompt with five lines of context will consistently outperform a clever prompt with none.

"Context is not extra. It is the work. The model is ready; your job is to give it something real to work with."
Principle three of three

One prompt, one job.

Do not use the same prompt for interviews, cover letters, and offer reviews. One prompt per task. Each one becomes sharper with use. Mixing tasks in one prompt forces Claude to guess your priority, and it will guess wrong.

"If Claude's answer feels off, the problem is almost always the prompt, not the model. Simplify, or split into two smaller prompts."
✓
Ready to move on when:
  • You understand that setup quality determines output quality, not the model.
  • You can explain the difference between role, context, and task.
  • You know why one project should not handle multiple unrelated tasks.
01
Lesson one · Build your project

Anatomy of a project,
built in five steps.

Every well-designed project separates information into three distinct blocks. Mixing them is the most common mistake. Get the structure right once, and every future project builds on the same template.

The structure

Three blocks. Each one has a different job.

Projects fail when people confuse instructions, goals, and task prompts: mixing all three into one long block. The separation is what makes the system work.

Block one
Instructions

How Claude should behave. Tone, language, level of detail, priorities, and what not to do. This block does most of the heavy lifting.

Block two
The Goal

One short sentence. The main thing you are trying to achieve. No more. It anchors every conversation to a purpose.

Block three
Task prompts

A specific prompt for each situation: reviewing an offer, writing a cover letter, prepping an interview. One per task.

Why separation matters

Mixing them forces Claude to guess.

If your instructions say "be concise" and your task prompt says "write a detailed breakdown," Claude has to guess which rule wins. Keep the how separate from the what and this ambiguity disappears completely.

Build your Job Coach project: step by step
1
Go to claude.ai and create a new Project.

In the left sidebar, find the Projects section. Click + Create project. Name it clearly, for example Job Coach · DACH. Specific names prevent confusion if you create more projects later.

2
Write your Instructions: Block one.

Inside the project, click Edit project instructions. Paste your instructions block here. This is where you define Claude's role, your market, your language preference, your target roles, and what you do not want.

Example: "You are my Job Coach for the DACH and EMEA job market. Help me review job offers, write applications, and practice interviews. Be direct. Do not invent information. Reply in English by default; in German if the input is German. Target roles: Senior CSM, Account Management. Not interested in: pure sales, relocation outside DACH."
3
Add knowledge files: your context layer.

Under Project knowledge, click Add content. Drop in your CV, a list of target companies, or tone-of-voice notes. Claude will reference these automatically in every conversation inside this project, without you having to paste them each time.

4
Start a conversation: Claude already knows you.

Open a New chat inside the project. Paste a job description and ask for a review. Notice that Claude does not ask who you are or what your target market is. The project carries that context automatically into every conversation.

✓
Keep iterating. Projects are living documents.

If an answer feels off, edit your instructions rather than rewriting the same prompt every time. Refine every few weeks as you learn what works. The best projects are edited more than they are written.

✓
Ready to move on when:
  • You have created at least one project with instructions and a knowledge file.
  • You have run a conversation inside the project and noticed it remembered your context.
  • You understand that instructions, knowledge, and task prompts are three separate things.
02
Lesson two · Styles & preferences

The settings nobody
touches, but should.

Almost no one configures these. They are the single easiest way to stop responses sounding like AI. Set them once and they apply across every project and chat you ever open.

Where to find them

Profile & preferences: two controls that matter.

Inside Claude's settings panel, go to Profile. You will find fields for your name, your role, the traits you want Claude to have, and your default response style.

Think of these as your personal defaults. Project instructions are the job-specific overrides. The two layers stack, and together they eliminate generic, impersonal output.

"These settings apply everywhere, on top of your project instructions. Fill them once. Benefit from them in every future chat, forever."
Configure the two levers that matter most
i
Traits: this is where you kill the AI voice.

Describe the kind of colleague you want Claude to be. Be specific and blunt. This is not a place for politeness; it is a place for precision.

Example: "Direct. Push back when I'm wrong. Short sentences. No 'I'd be happy to help.' Use examples from Customer Success and EdTech when possible."
ii
Response style: pick Concise as your default.

You can always ask for depth in the moment, but you cannot un-read a 2,000-word answer you did not want. Set it to Concise and switch temporarily when you need a long-form draft.

Tip: if you are writing a cover letter or long document, switch to Explanatory for that session, then switch back to Concise afterward.
✓
Save and notice the difference immediately.

Open a new chat and ask Claude something simple. The difference in tone will be immediate. Your name, your background, and your preferences will shape the response without any extra setup on your part.

Practice exercise

Configure your preferences now, before lesson three.

Go to claude.ai → Profile → Preferences. Fill in your name, your role, your traits, and your response style. Write the traits the way you would brief a real colleague, not the way you would describe yourself in a job application.

What to notice after saving: Start a fresh chat outside any project and ask a work-related question. Notice whether the tone matches what you asked for. If it still feels generic, your traits description is too vague; make it more specific.
✓
Ready to move on when:
  • Your Profile is filled in with a name, role description, and specific traits.
  • Your default response style is set to Concise.
  • You have tested the difference with a real question.
03
Lesson three · Prompts

Four prompts
to copy today.

Same goal, same model, radically different results. The difference is five extra lines of context. Here is the before and after, then four ready-to-use prompts for your job search.

Before & after: the same request, two outcomes

Context is the work. Not the phrasing.

The prompt below left has a goal. The prompt below right has a goal and a role, a market, a scope, a tone, and permission to push back. That is the entire difference.

✗ Too vague
No role, no context, no rules

Claude has no market, no seniority, no language, no boundaries. The answer applies to everyone, and therefore to no one.

"Help me with my job search."
✓ What you want
Role, context, rules, and boundaries

Claude now has a role, a market, a scope, a tone, and a permission to be direct. Every answer will be more useful without extra typing next time.

"Act as my Job Coach for the German and European market. Help me review offers, write applications, and prep interviews. Be direct and realistic. If a role doesn't fit, say so."
Four prompts: paste these into your Job Coach project
Prompt 01 · Use when: you are deciding whether to apply
Reviewing a job offer
Here is a job post. Tell me: · Fit score (0–10) with one-line reason · Top 3 strengths I should highlight · Top 3 gaps and how to address them · Red flags in the posting itself · Your final recommendation: apply, skip, or clarify first [Paste JD below]
→ Run this before you write a single word of a cover letter. Five minutes of honest evaluation saves two hours of wasted effort.
Prompt 02 · Use when: you have decided to apply
Writing a cover letter
Draft a cover letter for this role. Rules: · Match the language of the job post · Professional but human tone, no clichés · Max 250 words · Open with a sharp hook, not "I am writing to apply" · Close with a concrete next step [Paste JD + link to my CV]
→ Your project instructions are already set. This prompt can stay short because Claude already knows your background and tone preferences.
Prompt 03 · Use when: you have an interview booked
Preparing for an interview
I have an interview for [role] at [company]. Prepare me: · 8 likely questions (mix behavioural + role-specific) · Sample STAR answers using my CV · 3 weak points they might probe + how I'd handle them · 3 sharp questions I should ask them Stage: [screening / hiring manager / final]
→ Always specify the stage. "First call with HR" and "final panel" need completely different preparation.
Prompt 04 · Use when: you want a warm intro or informational chat
Networking outreach
Write a networking message to [person, role, company]. Rules: · Under 80 words · Clear purpose (what I want, what I offer) · Warm but not sycophantic · Give me one LinkedIn version and one email version Context on why I'm reaching out: [1–2 lines]
→ In DACH, try German even when the job is advertised in English. Local recruiters respond at a significantly higher rate.
What makes these prompts work
They stay short because your project instructions carry the context. You do not repeat yourself; Claude already knows your background.
They give Claude a structure to follow (bullet points, word limits, explicit rules), so the output is predictable and usable without heavy editing.
They have boundaries ("no clichés", "do not invent information", "say so if it doesn't fit"), which is how you prevent generic, over-enthusiastic output.
One prompt, one job. Each prompt covers a single task. Mixing tasks in one prompt forces Claude to guess your priority, and it will guess wrong.
✓
Ready to move on when:
  • You have copied at least one prompt into your Job Coach project.
  • You understand why these prompts work without being long.
  • You have seen the difference between a vague and a structured prompt in practice.

Lesson four · Troubleshoot & template

When things go wrong,
and your starter kit.

If Claude's output feels generic, confused, or too long, the model is almost never the problem. Run through this checklist before you give up; then copy the template below to start your next project in minutes.

The troubleshooting principle

Bad output is almost always a setup problem.

The single most common mistake is a vague or contradictory instructions block. Before assuming the model is wrong, check whether the issue is in your setup. The checklist below covers the eight most frequent causes of poor output.

"If you are on turn 10 of the same conversation and things are drifting, start a fresh chat. Long threads accumulate noise. Your project instructions will still be there."
Troubleshooting checklist: click to tick off
Is the role clear? (Coach, reviewer, editor, strategist?)
Is the goal stated in one sentence?
Is the language defined? (English? German? match the input?)
Is the tone defined? (direct? warm? formal?)
Did you tell Claude what you don't want?
Are tasks separated, or jammed into one prompt?
Are there contradictions between instructions and the prompt?
If all else fails: simplify, or split into two smaller prompts.
Your copy-paste starter kit

Three blocks. Edit the brackets. Done.

The template below is all you need to start a new project. Paste Block one into Project Instructions, Block two into the goal field, and use the four prompts from Lesson 03 as your task prompts.

You do not need a perfect project on day one. Start rough, use it for a week, then refine.

Block one · Paste into Project Instructions
Instructions template: edit the bracketed parts
You are my Job Coach for the [DACH / EMEA / your market] job market. Help me review job offers, prepare applications, write messages, and practice interviews. Be direct, clear, and realistic. Do not invent information. If something does not fit, tell me tactfully but honestly. Reply in [English] by default. If the input is in [German], reply in [German]. Target roles: [list your target roles]. Not interested in: [list what you want to avoid].
→ This is Block one. Edit the brackets. Paste the entire block into Project Instructions.
Block two · Paste into "What are you trying to achieve"
Goal statement
Helping me search for jobs more efficiently: analyse offers, adapt my CV, write cover letters and outreach, prep interviews, and prioritise where to spend my energy.
→ One sentence. That is all this field needs. Do not write a paragraph here.
Final exercise

Set up your first complete project using this template.

Create a new project, paste Block one into Instructions, add Block two as the goal, drop in your CV as a knowledge file, and run the job review prompt from Lesson 03 with a real job description.

The complete setup takes under ten minutes. After that, every conversation inside this project starts with your full context already loaded. You never have to explain yourself again.
